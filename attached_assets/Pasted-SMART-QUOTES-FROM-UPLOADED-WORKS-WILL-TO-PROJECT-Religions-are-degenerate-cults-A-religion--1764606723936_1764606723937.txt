CHAPTER 13 (EMPIRICISM) 

Thoughts are taught by being elicited, not by being deposited. This is the most fundamental problem with empiricism.
No knowledge is strictly sense-based.
External objects don’t stock our minds; they only activate what is already in them.
Perception apprises us only of structure.
Spatiotemporal knowledge is comparative knowledge.
Observation tells you how things are, not how they should be.
Your senses give you intra-situational information, never inter-situational information.
If one used only sense-derived knowledge, one could not infer anything about anything.
Categories are not devices human beings created to expedite thought.
His categories don’t cut nature at the joints.
Secondary properties are not in any relevant sense “subjective.”
Smells track primary properties; they are nature’s early-warning system.
Temperature is a primary property; the unpleasantness is just a side effect.
What sentences really mean is not what their surface grammar suggests.
Berkeley failed to prove idealism, but he discovered the distinction between grammatical form and logical form.
Berkeley was the first model-theorist.
To be is not to be perceived; to be conceived is to consider a proposition, not an object.
Conventions are arbitrary, but consistency among conventions is not.
You can draw a unicorn, but you cannot draw the dependence of two on one and one.
The mind supplies the paint; the world only tells it when to paint.
A rock will never form an image, no matter how many photons you fire at it.
Sense-perception has two jobs: triggering and transmitting. Empiricism only sees the second.
You did not learn the concept of space from seeing spaces.
Truths of reason are not truths about irregular lines on blackboards.
113 + 876 = 989 is not known by counting pears and pineapples.
Arithmetic is grasped by the intellect, not assembled from experience.
All explanations presuppose universals; there is no explanation without them.
Two pints of water belong together; one pint of water and one pint of gasoline do not; nature has joints.
Similarity is not mind-dependent; it exists in worlds without minds.
Smoke is evidence of fire because smoke and fire are causally connected; universals have no causal powers, so nothing in experience is evidence of them.
Sense-perception discloses only particulars; it never justifies belief in laws.
The concept of causation cannot be imaged, therefore it cannot come from experience.
Berkeley’s “to be is to be conceived” fails because you cannot conceive an unconceived object; you can only consider the proposition that one exists.
Idealism is commonsense’s way of committing suicide while pretending to defend it.
